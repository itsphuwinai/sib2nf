Origins and History

For over two thousand years, the carnation has been a quiet witness to history, its petals carrying whispers of love and remembrance. In ancient Greece and Rome, it was cherished for its gentle fragrance and delicate form, often woven into crowns that graced solemn ceremonies. Its name is believed to stem from the Latin corone—a tribute to the flower garlands that adorned rituals, hearts, and time itself.

Pink Carnation

A bloom of soft gratitude, a whispered promise to never forget. Light pink, like a mother’s love—gentle, warm. Deep pink, the color of new affection, offered shyly on a first meeting.

Red Carnation

Deep red, like a heart that beats with love unfaltering. A softer red, an admiration left unspoken, an ache carried quietly.

White Carnation

A symbol of quiet devotion, a vow of loyalty that does not waver. A bloom that speaks of faithfulness, of love that endures beyond words.

Yellow Carnation

A petal tinged with sorrow, a bloom of farewell. Once, it was given as a quiet ending—a flower that carried the weight of goodbye.

Purple Carnation

A bloom as unpredictable as the wind—wild, free, untamed. In some places, it carries sorrow; in others, it whispers of success, of apologies, of wealth yet to come.

Striped Carnation

A flower of hesitation, of words left unsaid. Its petals, streaked with color, speak of regret—of a love that could not be, of an apology whispered too late.
